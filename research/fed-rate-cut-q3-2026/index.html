The Fed Will Not Cut Rates Before Q3 2026 — and the July 28–29 Meeting Offers No Relief Either

Macro Analysis · Fed Policy cluster · 8 min read

Two Readings of the Question, Two Clear Answers

Strict reading (before July 1, 2026): No — mechanically impossible. The June 16–17 FOMC meeting concluded with rates held at 3.50%–3.75% (unanimous 12-0 vote), and the next scheduled meeting is July 28–29, 2026 — which falls inside Q3, not before it. With 10 days remaining before July 1 and no emergency meeting signaled anywhere in the evidence, there is no mechanism by which a cut could occur in this window.

Loose reading (at the July 28–29 meeting): Almost certainly no. Every policy signal available as of June 20 — the dot plot, inflation data, Chair Warsh's communications, and market pricing — points against a cut at that meeting. The Fed's own median projection for end-2026 sits at 3.8%, 17–18 basis points above the current 3.625% midpoint, meaning the committee's central tendency is a modest tightening, not easing.

The most actionable takeaway: anyone positioning for a Fed cut before Q3 is fighting both the calendar and the committee's stated reaction function.

Why the Calendar Closes the Door on "Before Q3"

The FOMC held four meetings in H1 2026 — January 27–28, March 17–18, April 28–29, and June 16–17 — and maintained rates at 3.50%–3.75% at every one. The June 17 meeting was the last opportunity before July 1; it passed without a cut. The next meeting, July 28–29, falls on day 28 of Q3.

Emergency intermeeting rate changes do happen — the Fed used them during COVID-19 in March 2020 and during the 2008 financial crisis — but they require extraordinary economic dislocations. There is no evidence of any such condition being present or signaled: Q1 2026 GDP grew at 1.6% annualized, unemployment held at 4.3% with 172,000 payrolls added in May, and the NY Fed's recession probability model puts the 12-month-ahead probability at approximately 15%. None of these readings constitute an emergency-cut trigger.

The pre-Q3 question is therefore settled by scheduling, not probability. The more substantive question is whether the July 28–29 meeting — the first of Q3 — could deliver a cut, and what signals bear on that.

What the June 2026 SEP and Warsh's Posture Tell Us

Three signal categories, all pointing the same direction:

1. The dot plot: a hawkish revision, not a pivot

The June 2026 SEP median end-2026 rate projection rose to 3.8%, up from 3.4% in the March SEP — a 40-basis-point hawkish shift in three months. Of 18 participants submitting projections, 9 project at least one rate hike by year-end, 8 project rates steady or lower, and only 1 projects a cut. The 2027 median sits at 3.6%, implying the committee's own baseline has the first net easing arriving next year, not this year.

A rising median dot is not merely a forecast — it reflects the committee's aggregate reaction function: when the median moves up, the threshold data needed to justify a cut rises with it. The bar for the July 28–29 meeting is therefore higher than it was before June 17, not lower.

2. Inflation: 2.2 percentage points above target on the headline measure

May 2026 headline CPI came in at 4.2% year-over-year (up from 3.8% in April), and core CPI at 2.9% year-over-year. April PCE — the Fed's preferred measure — ran at 3.8% headline and 3.3% core. The SEP projects full-year 2026 headline PCE at 3.6%, with the 2% target not reached until 2027 on the SEP's own baseline. The gap between current headline CPI (4.2%) and the 2% target is 2.2 percentage points — not a rounding error that one or two data prints can close.

Warsh noted at the press conference that inflation has run well ahead of the 2% goal for more than five years. That framing matters operationally: it signals the committee is not treating current inflation as a transitory overshoot that a single good print would resolve.

3. Warsh's communications: the condition is explicit

Chair Warsh stated the Committee has dispensed with forward guidance entirely, calling it "not well-suited to the current policy conjuncture." He set one explicit condition for any policy shift: price stability — meaning the 2% target — must be reestablished. He also disclosed an internal split: half of participants believe rates should be at current levels or lower; half believe higher. That 50-50 split, combined with the majority-hike dot plot, means the dovish half of the committee does not have the votes to drive a near-term cut.

The April 2026 FOMC minutes add a second condition: rate reductions would be appropriate only once there are "clear indications that disinflation is firmly back on track or if solid signs emerge of labor market weakness." Neither condition is currently met — inflation is accelerating on the headline measure, and the labor market added 172,000 jobs in May with unemployment steady.

The One Dissenting Estimate: Why the 50–70% Probability Claim Doesn't Hold

One of the eight analyses in the evidence base assigned a "moderate probability (approximately 50–70%)" to a cut before Q3. This estimate is a significant outlier — the other seven analyses converge on "no" or "mechanically impossible."

Examining the underlying evidence for that analysis, it draws on October 2025 FOMC data (a rate reduction to 3.75%–4.00%) and JPMorgan forecasts from late 2025 — sources that predate the June 17, 2026 meeting outcome by roughly six months. The estimate was almost certainly constructed before the June 2026 SEP and Warsh's press conference were available. It is not a live market signal; it is a stale pre-meeting forecast that has since been superseded.

Trigger Conditions: What Would Change This Judgment

The "before Q3" verdict is closed. For the July 28–29 meeting and beyond, here are the specific data releases and thresholds that would shift the calculus:

Base case

Inflation remains above 3% through summer. The Fed holds at 3.50%–3.75% through at least the September 15–16 meeting. The SEP's own 2027 median of 3.6% implies the first net easing arrives next year. The May 2026 PCE release (scheduled June 25) and July CPI (scheduled July 14) will be the next two inflation prints before the July 28–29 meeting — neither is likely to show the kind of sharp reversal that would reopen the cut discussion.

Accelerated-cut scenario (illustrative)

Assume headline CPI drops to approximately 2.5% or below in the July data release (published mid-August), and unemployment rises 0.5 percentage points or more in a single month. Under those assumed inputs, the April minutes' second condition — "solid signs of labor market weakness" — would be triggered, and the September 15–16 meeting would become live for a cut. This scenario requires a disinflation pace roughly three times faster than the current trend; it is possible but not the base case.

Further-tightening scenario (illustrative)

Assume headline CPI re-accelerates above 5% and the September SEP dot plot median rises above 4%. The committee's hawkish half — already projecting hikes — would gain the votes to act. This scenario is constrained by the fact that the current rate (3.50%–3.75%) is already described by Warsh as "somewhat restrictive" in housing, limiting appetite for aggressive additional tightening.

Two data points to monitor actively:
  1. June 25 PCE release — the last major inflation print before the July 28–29 meeting. Any downside surprise would be the earliest factual basis for revising the base case.
  2. September 2026 SEP dot plot — if the median falls back toward 3.4% (the March 2026 level), that would signal the committee is beginning to price in a late-2026 cut; if it holds at 3.8% or rises further, the first cut moves into 2027.
